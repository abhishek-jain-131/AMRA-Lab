What We Do

At our lab, we specialize in surface modification for a variety of applications. Our research focuses on enhancing condensation processes, developing self-cleaning surfaces, studying droplet impact dynamics, and improving dispersed phase filtration. By leveraging advanced techniques and materials, we aim to create innovative solutions that address critical challenges in fields ranging from environmental engineering to biomedical diagnostics.

Some of our Projects

Here are a few of the current and previous projects that have been a part of AMRA Lab. While we do not have any imposed boundary conditions on our experiments, imagination and contribution to science, we do specialize in modifying surfaces to make new concepts in fluid sciences, thermal engineering and energy/water sustainability research.

Fabrication and characterization of transparent, self-cleaning glass covers for solar photovoltaic cells

A self-cleaning superhydrophobic coating for solar photovoltaic (SPV) panels, created by depositing silica nanoparticles and fluoroalkylsilane on glass, repels water and enables efficient cleaning with minimal water. The treated surface shows minimal impact on SPV voltage output and significantly improves performance recovery compared to untreated surfaces.

Droplet Morphology-Based Wettability Tuning and Design of Fog Harvesting Mesh to Minimize Mesh-Clogging

Fog harvesting uses a porous mesh to collect water from fog, but typical meshes face clogging and inefficiency issues. By optimizing mesh parameters such as pitch, shade coefficient, and fiber wettability, and studying different fiber wettabilities, the most effective design for clog-proof, hydrophobic meshes with square pores and optimal shade coefficients has been identified.

Wettability patterning for high-rate, pumpless fluid transport on open, non-planar microfluidic platforms

Surface tension-driven transport on open substrates facilitates low-cost biomedical diagnostics by using wettability-patterned microfluidic tracks that move liquid volumes (1-500 μL) without power input. Using TiO2-based coatings, these tracks enable complex droplet handling and fluid transport rates up to 350 μL/s, demonstrating applications in both diagnostic devices and microscale engineering, such as fuel cell water management.

Enhancing Dropwise Condensation through Bioinspired Wettability Patterning

This study introduces a technique to control the maximum diameter (Dmax) of condensate droplets in dropwise condensation (DWC) on vapor/air surfaces, enhancing heat transfer rates. By using laser-patterned hydrophilic-superhydrophilic strips on aluminum, the method increases condensate collection by up to 19%, with a bioinspired interdigitated pattern outperforming other designs, especially under high humidity and noncondensable gas conditions.

Heat transfer augmentation using a magnetic fluid under the influence of a line dipole

Ferrofluids show potential for heat transfer applications due to the control of advective transport via external magnetic fields. This study simulates two-dimensional forced convection in a channel influenced by a line-source dipole magnetic field, revealing localized magnetic effects that enhance heat transfer through the creation of local vortices and recirculation zones. Heat transfer enhancement depends on the magnetizing current and dipole placement, allowing predictions based on magnetic moment and dipole distribution.

Self-Driven One-Step Oil Removal from Oil Spill on Water via Selective-Wettability Steel Mesh

A new surface-tension-driven, gravity-assisted method for oil-water separation uses a superhydrophobic and superoleophilic stainless steel mesh to efficiently collect oil from floating spills without preseparation pumping. The benchtop prototype achieves over 94% collection efficiency for oils of varying viscosities and demonstrates high stability and scalability for large-scale cleanup efforts.

Energy and exergy analyses of an externally fired gas turbine (EFGT) cycle integrated with biomass gasifier for distributed power generation

Biomass-based decentralized power generation using an externally fired gas turbine (EFGT) is technically feasible, with thermal performance influenced by cycle pressure ratio, turbine inlet temperature (TIT), and heat exchanger cold end temperature difference (CETD). Maximum thermal efficiency occurs at an optimum pressure ratio, increasing with higher TIT or lower CETD. A 100 kW EFGT plant's performance analysis identifies a trade-off in operating conditions.

Key design and operating parameters for enhancing dropwise condensation through wettability patterning

We present a scalable method to enhance condensate removal and heat transfer on vertical aluminum plates during water vapor condensation with non-condensable gases. By strategically placing superhydrophilic tracks on a hydrophilic surface, we facilitate capillary-driven drainage and improved dropwise condensation, achieving over 30% heat transfer improvement. This design optimizes condensate management and enhances overall heat transfer performance.

Analyzing ferrofluid transport for magnetic drug targeting

Experimental and numerical investigations of magnetically induced localization of ferrofluid and its subsequent dispersion are performed in a forced flow. The ferrofluid accumulation behaves as a solid obstacle in the flow as the competing magnetic and fluid shear forces give rise to a rigidly bound core region followed by a washaway region at its wake. Results of the analysis provide meaningful information on ferrofluid transport for various magnetic drug targeting applications.

Experimental investigation of thermophysical properties of Al2O3–water nanofluid: Role of surfactants

Nanofluids, colloidal suspensions of nanoparticles, enhance heat transfer and are studied for their stability and thermophysical properties. This work investigates Al2O3-water nanofluids, examining the effects of temperature (20-60 °C), solid volume fractions (0.1-2.0%), and surfactants, finding sodium dodecylbenzenesulfonate (SDBS) offers the best stabilization. The study provides a comprehensive thermophysical property database, showing that viscosity increases with solid volume fraction and decreases with temperature, while thermal conductivity increases with both.

Synthesis and characterization of TiO2–water nanofluids with different surfactants

This study characterizes the viscosity, thermal conductivity, surface tension, and pH of TiO2-water nanofluid across temperatures (20-60°C) and solid volume fractions (0.1%-2.0%). Stability is achieved with CTAB and acetic acid surfactants. Results show viscosity increases with solid volume fraction and decreases with temperature, while thermal conductivity increases with both. Surface tension decreases with higher solid volume fractions and temperature, and pH remains stable.

The effects of magnetic nanoparticle properties on magnetic fluid hyperthermia

Magnetic fluid hyperthermia (MFH) uses ferrofluid-impregnated tumors heated by an AC magnetic field to selectively destroy cancer cells while sparing healthy tissue. Iron-platinum, magnetite, and maghemite nanoparticles are promising MFH candidates, providing controlled heating that effectively targets tumor centers while keeping surrounding tissues at safe temperatures.

Analytical model for the magnetophoretic capture of magnetic microspheres in microfluidic devices

This paper proposes an analytical technique for magnetophoretic transport of functionalized magnetic microspheres in micro-total-analysis systems (μTAS). It highlights the importance of magnetic field strength and particle size in achieving successful capture under pressure-driven or electroosmotic flow through microchannels. The study characterizes separator performance using non-dimensional parameters λ and γ, aiding in optimal design and operation for desired capture efficiency in microfluidic separators.

Cooling tower fog harvesting in power plants – A pilot study

Fog harvesting from cooling tower plumes presents a novel approach to combat water scarcity, potentially recovering up to 40% of water lost as drift in thermal power plants. This innovative method, utilizing specific mesh parameters and inclinations to enhance collection efficiency, offers a promising solution to industrial water losses, exceeding efficiencies of traditional fog collectors and paving the way for broader applications in artificial fog generators.

Single magnetic particle dynamics in a microchannel

In micrototal analysis systems, functionalized magnetic particles navigate microfluidic channels under nonuniform magnetic fields, facilitating their controlled collection for bioanalytical processes. Collection efficiency hinges on dimensionless ratios assessing magnetic and particle drag forces relative to magnetic field strength, particle size, susceptibility, host fluid velocity, viscosity, and channel dimensions.

A scaling analysis to characterize thermomagnetic convection

Thermomagnetic convection in a square enclosure filled with ferrofluid, influenced by an external magnetic field from a line dipole, shows that the height-averaged Nusselt number scales with the magnetic Rayleigh number. Numerical simulations validate this scaling, with the Langevin equation identifying an optimal enclosure height maximizing the Nusselt number for a given dipole strength.

Magnetic microsphere-based mixers for microdroplets

In droplet-based microfluidic systems, achieving efficient reagent mixing is enhanced through active magnetic stirring using self-assembled chains of magnetic microspheres. This method utilizes a rotating magnetic field to optimize mixing rates, evaluated by a dimensional mixing parameter dependent on field strength, RPM, fluid viscosity, and microsphere concentration, identifying a critical Mason number for optimal mixing under varied conditions.

Microfluidic transport in magnetic MEMS and bioMEMS

Magnetic nanoparticles and microparticles, like ferrimagnetic and ferromagnetic variants in ferrofluids, are pivotal in MEMS and bioMEMS due to their tunable magnetic properties and large functionalization areas. They enable precise manipulation in microfluidic systems through external magnetic fields, facilitating diverse biochemical applications and enhancing micro-total analysis systems (µ-TAS) capabilities.

Cell separation in a microfluidic channel using magnetic microspheres

Numerical analysis investigates magnetophoretic cell separation in microfluidic channels using magnetic microspheres and a line dipole. It explores fluid flow, particle trajectories, and capture efficiency under varying parameters like dipole strength, particle properties, and channel configuration, offering insights for optimizing microfluidic cell separators in biomedicine and biotechnology.
